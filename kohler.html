Key principle: Do not compete head-to-head on US coverage. Position European expertise as the differentiator. Be open to a complementary role.

Key principle: Complete honesty. Do not oversell or make vague promises. The phased approach aligns with Kohler's own building timeline.

"We already have a PIM system / content syndication tool"

Response: A PIM or syndication tool manages what you send out. Digital shelf analytics monitors what actually shows up on the other end - and the two are complementary, not overlapping. Think of it as the feedback loop: your PIM ensures you are sending accurate, complete product data. Digital shelf analytics tells you whether that data actually rendered correctly on the retailer's site, how it compares to competitors, and whether customers are finding it. For a company distributing 3D models through VNTANA to Amazon, Home Depot, and Lowe's, the question is: are those assets appearing as intended on each retailer's platform? That is what DSA answers.

Key principle: Position DSA as complementary to existing tech stack, not competitive with it.

"We handle this internally / our team does manual spot checks"

Response: Manual spot checks work when the scope is manageable. The challenge scales with the catalogue - when a single faucet model exists in 9+ finishes across 27+ retailers, that is hundreds of product pages for one product line. Multiply that across 15,000-30,000 SKUs and 10+ brands, and manual monitoring becomes impractical to maintain consistently. The question is less about whether the team can do it and more about whether their time is better spent acting on insights rather than gathering data. We see brands transition from manual to automated monitoring not because manual was wrong, but because the team's capacity is better used elsewhere once the catalogue reaches a certain scale.

Key principle: Respect the existing approach. Frame automation as a scale solution, not a replacement for human judgment.
